## 1. The CSFD Plot (Cumulative Size-Frequency Distribution)

**File:** `outputs/plots/csfd_plot_FULL.png`

### What are you looking at?
The CSFD plot is a standard tool in planetary science used to estimate surface age. It is displayed on a **log-log scale**.

- **X-Axis:** Crater Diameter (D)  
- **Y-Axis:** Cumulative density (N), representing the number of craters ≥ D per km²  

### Analytical Breakdown: Why does the graph look like this?

#### 1. Downward Slope (Power-Law Behavior)
The plot follows a downward diagonal trend. This reflects a **power-law distribution** of impact events:  
- Small impactors are far more frequent than large ones  
- As diameter increases, crater frequency decreases exponentially  

#### 2. Left-Side Rollover (Detection Limit)
At very small diameters (~1.5–2.5 m), the curve flattens.

- This does **not** indicate absence of small craters  
- It reflects the resolution limit (~0.5 m/pixel)  
- Craters near 1 m span only ~2 pixels → unreliable detection  

This region defines the **minimum reliable detection threshold** of the system.

#### 3. Vertical Error Bars (Poisson Statistics)
Each data point includes vertical uncertainty:

- Computed using: ±√N / Area  
- Small craters → large counts → low uncertainty  
- Large craters → small counts → high uncertainty

## 2. The Density Heatmap

**File:** `outputs/plots/density_heatmap_FULL.png`

### What are you looking at?
A 2D spatial distribution of crater density:

- Dark blue/purple → low crater density (smooth terrain)  
- Yellow/red → high crater density (rough terrain)  

### Analytical Breakdown: Geological Interpretation

#### 1. Uneven Clustering (Secondary Cratering)
Clusters or hotspots indicate **secondary craters**:

- Large impacts eject debris over long distances  
- Ejected material lands in concentrated regions  
- Produces clustered crater patterns  

#### 2. Smooth Regions (Resurfacing Events)
Low-density regions suggest geological resurfacing:

- Lava flows (mare basalt) covering older craters  
- Ejecta blankets from nearby impacts  

#### 3. Practical Application
Such maps are used for:

- Landing site selection  
- Hazard analysis for autonomous spacecraft

## 3. The Visual HUD Overlay

**File:** `outputs/overlays/research_overlay_FULL.png`

### What are you looking at?
A high-resolution image with detected craters overlaid as circular markers.

### Analytical Breakdown: Visual Verification

#### 1. Sub-Pixel Accuracy
Detected circles align with the full crater rim (shadow + highlight), not just dark regions.

- Confirms effectiveness of the contrast-based fitting method  
- Demonstrates sub-pixel precision  

#### 2. Missing or Rejected Craters
Some visually apparent craters may not be marked:

- May be classified as non-crater features (e.g., boulders)  
- May fail contrast-based freshness criteria  
- May not meet multi-view consistency requirements  

#### 3. Size Distribution Insight
Large craters containing many smaller ones visually confirm:

- Hierarchical impact structure  
- Power-law behavior observed in CSFD

The outputs of the CraterX pipeline align with established planetary science principles:

- **CSFD plots** confirm power-law impact distributions  
- **Heatmaps** reveal spatial clustering due to secondary impacts  
- **Overlay visualizations** validate geometric and sub-pixel accuracy  

Together, these results demonstrate both the scientific validity and computational robustness of the system.
